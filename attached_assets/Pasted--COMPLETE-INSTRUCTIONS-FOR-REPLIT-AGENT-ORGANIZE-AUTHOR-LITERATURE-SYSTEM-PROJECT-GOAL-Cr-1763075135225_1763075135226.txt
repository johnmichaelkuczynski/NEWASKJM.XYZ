## FILE SPECIFICATIONS

### 1. primary_texts.txt

**Purpose:** Raw primary source material - full texts or substantial excerpts from the author's work.

**Format:**
```
[WORK: Title of Work]
[DATE: Publication date]
[SECTION: Chapter/Part name if applicable]

[Full text or substantial excerpt here]

---

[WORK: Next Title]
[DATE: Publication date]
[SECTION: Chapter/Part name if applicable]

[Full text or substantial excerpt here]

---
```

**Instructions:**
- Include major works in full when possible
- Include significant excerpts from longer works
- Use standard published translations for non-English works
- Preserve original formatting, paragraph breaks
- Separate works with "---"

---

### 2. style_guide.txt

**Purpose:** Document the author's distinctive writing style, sentence patterns, rhetorical devices, and narrative techniques. This file is CRITICAL for fiction generation functions.

**Required Sections:**

```
AUTHOR: [Name]

SENTENCE STRUCTURE:
- [Describe typical sentence patterns]
- [Note preferred sentence length - short/medium/long]
- [Characteristic clause structures]
- [How they handle complex ideas syntactically]

PARAGRAPH ORGANIZATION:
- [Typical paragraph length]
- [How they structure arguments within paragraphs]
- [Use of topic sentences]
- [Transition strategies]

VOCABULARY PREFERENCES:
- [Level of formality: academic/conversational/literary]
- [Technical vs. ordinary language use]
- [Characteristic words and phrases they use frequently]
- [Words/concepts they avoid]

RHETORICAL DEVICES:
- [List specific devices: metaphor, analogy, rhetorical questions, etc.]
- [How they use examples]
- [How they introduce counterarguments]
- [How they emphasize key points]

TONE:
- [Formal/informal, serious/playful, didactic/exploratory, etc.]
- [Emotional register - passionate, detached, ironic, etc.]
- [Relationship to reader - authoritative, conversational, challenging]

NARRATIVE STRUCTURES (if applicable):
- [For fiction writers: plot structures, character development approaches]
- [For philosophers: dialogue, essay, treatise, aphorism, case study, etc.]
- [How they open pieces]
- [How they conclude]

HANDLING COUNTERARGUMENTS:
- [How they introduce objections]
- [How they refute opposing views]
- [Whether they're charitable or dismissive]

USE OF EXAMPLES:
- [Concrete vs. abstract]
- [Historical, contemporary, hypothetical]
- [Scientific, everyday, literary]

DISTINCTIVE STYLISTIC MARKERS:
- [Any unique tics, habits, recurring phrases]
- [Signature moves that identify this author]
```

**EXAMPLE - BERKELEY:**
```
AUTHOR: George Berkeley

SENTENCE STRUCTURE:
- Uses "It may be objected that..." to introduce counterarguments
- Frequently employs rhetorical questions to guide reader
- Moderate sentence length, clear and direct
- Uses subordinate clauses to qualify claims carefully

PARAGRAPH ORGANIZATION:
- Short to medium paragraphs
- Typically: state position → anticipate objection → refute with example
- Strong topic sentences
- Uses "But..." or "However..." frequently for transitions

VOCABULARY PREFERENCES:
- Accessible 18th-century English
- Avoids excessive Latin terminology
- Uses everyday objects as examples (tables, chairs, trees)
- Key terms: "perceive," "idea," "spirit," "matter"

RHETORICAL DEVICES:
- Socratic questioning - leads reader through logical steps
- Appeals to direct experience and observation
- Reductio ad absurdum of opposing positions
- Uses concrete sensory examples

TONE:
- Conversational yet philosophical
- Patient, pedagogical
- Sometimes gently mocking of materialist positions
- Earnest about religious implications

NARRATIVE STRUCTURES:
- Dialogue format (Three Dialogues Between Hylas and Philonous)
- Essay format (Principles of Human Knowledge)
- Always moves from common sense to philosophical conclusion

HANDLING COUNTERARGUMENTS:
- Always introduces them fairly: "It may be said..."
- Responds systematically
- Uses thought experiments to expose contradictions

USE OF EXAMPLES:
- Heavily concrete: colors, sounds, physical objects
- Appeals to immediate sensory experience
- Uses examples from daily life, not esoteric scenarios

DISTINCTIVE STYLISTIC MARKERS:
- "esse est percipi" (to be is to be perceived)
- Frequent appeals to what "any man" can observe
- Contrast between "vulgar" (common sense) and "philosophical" understanding
- Uses "I say" to emphasize key claims
```

**EXAMPLE - FREUD:**
```
AUTHOR: Sigmund Freud

SENTENCE STRUCTURE:
- Complex, Germanic sentence construction even in translation
- Long sentences with multiple subordinate clauses
- Careful qualifications and hedging
- Uses parenthetical asides frequently

PARAGRAPH ORGANIZATION:
- Long, dense paragraphs
- Builds arguments through accumulated evidence
- Returns cyclically to key themes
- Uses numbered lists for case details

VOCABULARY PREFERENCES:
- Technical psychoanalytic terminology (ego, id, superego, cathexis, libido)
- Medical/clinical language
- Archaeological metaphors (layers, excavation, buried material)
- Germanic compound constructions

RHETORICAL DEVICES:
- Case study method
- Interprets dreams, slips, symptoms as symbolic
- Uses analogies extensively (psyche as archaeology, as hydraulics)
- Appeals to clinical observation
- Anticipates skepticism explicitly

TONE:
- Clinical yet intimate
- Detached observer who reveals private matters
- Authoritative but acknowledges uncertainty
- Sometimes defensive about controversial claims

NARRATIVE STRUCTURES:
- Case history format: presenting complaint → biographical background → analysis of symptoms → interpretation → outcome
- First presents manifest content, then reveals latent content
- Dreams as central evidence
- Emphasizes resistance and transference

HANDLING COUNTERARGUMENTS:
- Acknowledges objections seriously
- Often interprets resistance to theory as evidence for theory
- Uses clinical data to counter theoretical objections
- Sometimes ad hominem: resistance indicates neurosis

USE OF EXAMPLES:
- Extended case studies (Dora, Rat Man, Wolf Man)
- Dreams with detailed associations
- Slips of the tongue, forgetting
- Mythology and literature (Oedipus)
- Personal observations from practice

DISTINCTIVE STYLISTIC MARKERS:
- "We are compelled to assume..."
- "Further analysis revealed..."
- "The patient's resistance at this point was significant..."
- Use of initials or pseudonyms (Herr K., Fräulein Elisabeth)
- Systematic return to childhood origins
- Sexual/aggressive interpretations
```

**EXAMPLE - WILLIAM JAMES:**
```
AUTHOR: William James

SENTENCE STRUCTURE:
- Varied: short emphatic sentences mixed with longer exploratory ones
- Conversational rhythm
- Uses dashes frequently for asides and qualifications
- Direct address to reader ("Consider...", "We find...")

PARAGRAPH ORGANIZATION:
- Medium length, reader-friendly
- Exploratory rather than dogmatic
- Often circles back to refine initial statement
- Comfortable with ambiguity

VOCABULARY PREFERENCES:
- American vernacular mixed with philosophical terminology
- Concrete, vivid language
- Psychological terms from introspection
- Avoids excessive abstraction
- Uses "cash value" idiom

RHETORICAL DEVICES:
- Thought experiments
- Introspective reports ("When I examine my own consciousness...")
- Pragmatic test: what practical difference does this make?
- Generous to opposing views before critiquing

TONE:
- Warm, conversational, personal
- Self-deprecating humor
- Genuinely curious and open
- Anti-dogmatic
- Intimate relationship with reader

NARRATIVE STRUCTURES:
- Essay format with personal reflection
- Often first-person: "I must confess..."
- Presents competing views fairly before offering resolution
- Emphasizes lived experience over abstract theory

HANDLING COUNTERARGUMENTS:
- Extremely charitable
- Often says opponents have legitimate point
- Pragmatic resolution: both sides partly right
- "The truth lies somewhere between..."

USE OF EXAMPLES:
- Personal experience and introspection
- Psychological case studies
- Religious experience narratives
- Everyday situations
- Scientific experiments

DISTINCTIVE STYLISTIC MARKERS:
- "The pragmatic method..."
- "What difference would it make?"
- "I must confess..."
- "Now, what does this mean in practice?"
- Emphasis on "feeling" and phenomenology
- "Stream of consciousness" metaphor
- "The cash value of..."
```

**EXAMPLE - JOHN-MICHAEL KUCZYNSKI:**
```
AUTHOR: John-Michael Kuczynski

SENTENCE STRUCTURE:
- Contemporary academic philosophy style
- Crisp, clear declarative sentences
- Uses em-dashes for asides and specifications
- Minimal subordination - prefers coordination or separate sentences
- Direct and unhedged

PARAGRAPH ORGANIZATION:
- Short, focused paragraphs
- Each paragraph makes one specific point
- Moves fast - no repetition
- Clear logical progression
- Often single-sentence paragraphs for emphasis

VOCABULARY PREFERENCES:
- Analytic philosophy terminology
- Precise technical language where needed
- Ordinary language for examples
- Mathematical/logical precision
- Avoids jargon when ordinary words suffice

RHETORICAL DEVICES:
- Falsification - presents hypothesis, shows it's wrong
- Reductio ad absurdum
- Concrete counterexamples
- Parsimonious explanations
- "What's philosophically interesting:" marker

TONE:
- Direct, no-nonsense
- Intellectually aggressive
- Impatient with imprecision
- Confident
- Sometimes dismissive of conventional wisdom

NARRATIVE STRUCTURES:
- Case study format for fiction
- States claim → tests claim → falsifies or confirms
- Uses contemporary scenarios
- Clinical detail without emotional editorializing
- Philosophical analysis after narrative

HANDLING COUNTERARGUMENTS:
- "You might object..."
- Addresses objection directly
- Shows why it fails
- No hedging or diplomatic language

USE OF EXAMPLES:
- Contemporary, concrete scenarios
- Named individuals with specific circumstances
- Detailed behavioral descriptions
- Emphasizes what's falsifiable/testable

DISTINCTIVE STYLISTIC MARKERS:
- "Consider X, age Y..."
- "What's philosophically interesting:"
- "The prediction is falsified."
- "This fails."
- "The more parsimonious explanation:"
- Uses actual numbers and specific details
- "contra the theory"
```

**EXAMPLE - BERKELEY:**
```
AUTHOR: George Berkeley

CORE PHILOSOPHICAL COMMITMENTS:
- Idealism: only minds (spirits) and ideas exist
- "To be is to be perceived" (esse est percipi)
- No mind-independent material substance
- God perceives everything constantly, ensuring continuity
- Direct perception of ideas, not of matter
- Rejection of abstract ideas
- Christian theism essential to system

WHAT THIS AUTHOR WOULD SAY:
- Material objects are collections of ideas
- Skepticism dissolves once we reject matter
- Common sense supports idealism, not materialism
- Science describes patterns in our perceptions
- God's perception grounds objective reality
- We know other minds by analogy with our own

WHAT THIS AUTHOR WOULD NOT SAY:
- Would not accept solipsism (God's mind ensures shared reality)
- Would not deny external world (ideas are real)
- Would not embrace atheism or deism
- Would not accept abstract ideas exist
- Would not say we can conceive matter
- Would not use substance dualism (mind + matter)

INTELLECTUAL PERSONALITY:
- Confident but patient
- Genuinely thinks his view is common sense
- Sees materialists as creating pseudo-problems
- Religious motivations central
- Pedagogical - wants to lead reader step by step

RELATIONSHIP TO OTHER THINKERS:
- INFLUENCED BY: Locke (but radicalizes his empiricism)
- OPPOSES: Locke's material substance, Descartes' dualism, materialism generally
- CONTEMPORARY OF: Hume (but more religious)
- TRADITION: British empiricism, but idealist

HISTORICAL CONTEXT:
- Early 18th century (1685-1753)
- Response to Newtonian science and materialism
- Defense of religion against mechanical philosophy
- Irish Anglican bishop

BLIND SPOTS / LIMITATIONS:
- Overestimates how intuitive idealism is
- Struggles with intersubjective agreement (God solves too easily)
- Can't easily explain difference between real vs. imagined perceptions
- Modern neuroscience undermines the mental/material distinction he relies on

TYPICAL PROBLEM-SOLVING APPROACH:
1. Identify opponent's position
2. Show it leads to skepticism or incoherence
3. Argue his idealism avoids these problems
4. Appeal to direct experience
5. Show consistency with common sense AND science

KEY CONCEPTS / TERMINOLOGY:
- Ideas: immediate objects of perception
- Spirit/Mind: that which perceives
- Matter: incoherent concept, impossible to conceive
- Perception: direct awareness of ideas
- Esse est percipi: to be is to be perceived
- Sensible qualities: colors, sounds, etc. are ideas not properties of matter
```

**EXAMPLE - FREUD:**
```
AUTHOR: Sigmund Freud

CORE PHILOSOPHICAL COMMITMENTS:
- Psychological determinism: all mental events have causes
- The unconscious is primary; consciousness is derivative
- Sexual and aggressive drives (libido and death drive) fundamental
- Childhood experiences shape adult psychology
- Dreams as wish-fulfillment
- Neurotic symptoms as symbolic/meaningful
- Pleasure principle vs. reality principle
- Id, ego, superego structure
- Repression as core defense mechanism
- Transference reveals unconscious patterns

WHAT THIS AUTHOR WOULD SAY:
- "Your feelings toward X remind you unconsciously of your relationship with your father/mother"
- "This dream symbolizes a wish that cannot be acknowledged"
- "The intensity of your denial suggests proximity to unconscious truth"
- "Neurotic symptoms are compromises between repressed wishes and defense"
- "Free association will reveal hidden meanings"
- "Resistance indicates we're approaching something important"
- "Early sexual experiences were traumatic and repressed"

WHAT THIS AUTHOR WOULD NOT SAY:
- Would not accept purely biological explanations without psychological meaning
- Would not deny sexual/aggressive motives behind behavior
- Would not accept conscious explanations at face value
- Would not embrace mysticism or spirituality (saw religion as illusion)
- Would not accept Adler's power drive or Jung's collective unconscious as primary
- Would not say trauma memories are inherently unreliable
- Would not abandon sexual etiology of neuroses

INTELLECTUAL PERSONALITY:
- Authoritative, sometimes dogmatic
- Deeply curious about hidden meanings
- Suspicious of surface explanations
- Willing to be controversial
- Values clinical observation over theory
- Protective of psychoanalytic orthodoxy
- Pessimistic about human nature and culture

RELATIONSHIP TO OTHER THINKERS:
- INFLUENCED BY: Charcot (hysteria), Breuer (talking cure), Darwin (drives/instincts)
- DISCIPLES WHO BROKE AWAY: Jung (spirituality), Adler (power), Rank (birth trauma)
- OPPOSES: Academic psychology, religious explanations, biological reductionism
- TRADITION: Medical/scientific approach to mind, but created new paradigm

HISTORICAL CONTEXT:
- Late 19th/early 20th century Vienna
- Bourgeois Victorian sexual repression
- Jewish in anti-Semitic culture
- Neurologist turned psychologist
- Fled Nazis in 1938

BLIND SPOTS / LIMITATIONS:
- Overemphasizes sexual etiology
- Male-centered psychology (penis envy, etc.)
- Limited sample (neurotic Viennese bourgeoisie)
- Confirmation bias (interprets resistance as confirmation)
- Some theories unfalsifiable
- Underestimates social/cultural factors
- Modern neuroscience challenges some assumptions

TYPICAL PROBLEM-SOLVING APPROACH:
1. Listen to patient's narrative and complaints
2. Note symbolic content, dreams, slips, resistances
3. Trace current symptoms to childhood experiences
4. Identify repressed wishes and defenses
5. Interpret symbolic meanings
6. Work through transference
7. Make the unconscious conscious

KEY CONCEPTS / TERMINOLOGY:
- Unconscious: repressed wishes and memories outside awareness
- Repression: pushing unacceptable thoughts out of consciousness
- Id: primitive drives and wishes
```

**EXAMPLE - MARXIST_FRAMEWORK.TXT:**
```
AUTHOR: Karl Marx
FRAMEWORK: Historical Materialism / Marxist Analysis

CORE ANALYTICAL STRUCTURE:
Base/superstructure model:
- MATERIAL BASE: Economic relations, mode of production, who owns means of production
- IDEOLOGICAL SUPERSTRUCTURE: Culture, law, religion, philosophy, art - justified by and serving the base
- DIALECTICAL PROCESS: Contradictions in base lead to revolutionary transformation
- CLASS CONFLICT: Primary engine of historical change

KEY CONCEPTS:
- Mode of production: How society produces goods (slavery, feudalism, capitalism, socialism)
- Means of production: Tools, factories, land, technology used to produce
- Relations of production: Who owns means vs. who labors
- Class: Position in relation to means of production (owners vs. workers)
- Exploitation: Owners extract surplus value from workers' labor
- Alienation: Workers separated from products, labor, species-being, others
- Ideology: Ideas that justify existing power relations
- False consciousness: Workers accepting ideology against their interests
- Class struggle: Conflict between owners and workers driving history
- Commodity fetishism: Social relations appear as relations between things
- Surplus value: Value produced by workers beyond their wages
- Dialectical materialism: Change through contradiction and resolution

ANALYTICAL PATTERN:
1. Identify the material base (who owns what, who does what labor)
2. Identify the superstructure (ideas, culture, laws)
3. Show how superstructure serves/justifies base
4. Identify contradictions in current mode of production
5. Predict revolutionary transformation when contradictions become unbearable
6. Identify whose interests are served by existing arrangements

APPLICABLE DOMAINS:
- Economics (obviously)
- Politics and law
- Religion ("opiate of the masses")
- Philosophy and ideology
- Art and culture
- Family structures
- Education systems
- Technology
- Urban planning
- Gender relations
- Race relations
- Environmental issues
- Any social institution

CHARACTERISTIC QUESTIONS:
- Who owns the means of production here?
- Who benefits materially from this arrangement?
- What ideology justifies this arrangement?
- What contradictions exist in this system?
- How is surplus value extracted?
- What would revolutionary transformation look like?
- Whose class interests does this serve?
- How are workers alienated in this domain?

EXAMPLE APPLICATIONS:

EXAMPLE 1 - EDUCATION:
Material base: Schools funded by property taxes (wealthy areas = better schools)
Relations: Wealthy control educational resources; working class labors to produce educated workers
Superstructure: Ideology of "meritocracy" - school success reflects individual talent
Function: System reproduces class relations - wealthy children get better education, better jobs, become new ruling class
Contradiction: Claims to be meritocratic but systematically advantages the already-advantaged
Class interest: Serves ruling class by providing them advantages while maintaining illusion of fairness
Alienation: Education becomes credential-gathering rather than intellectual development

EXAMPLE 2 - SOCIAL MEDIA:
Material base: Users produce content (labor), platform owners monetize it
Means of production: Algorithms, servers, user attention owned by tech companies
Relations: Users = workers, company = owners extracting surplus value
Exploitation: Users generate value through posts/engagement, receive no payment, companies profit from advertising
Alienation: Users' social connections mediated and controlled by platform
Superstructure: Ideology of "sharing economy," "connecting people," "democratizing media"
Contradiction: Celebrates user agency while extracting value from unpaid labor
Revolutionary potential: Users could collectively own platforms (worker cooperatives)

EXAMPLE 3 - ACADEMIC PUBLISHING:
Material base: Researchers produce knowledge, publishers control distribution
Means of production: Journals, peer review systems owned by for-profit companies
Relations: Researchers provide free labor (writing, reviewing), universities pay massive subscription fees, publishers profit
Exploitation: Publicly-funded research given free to publishers who sell it back to public institutions
Superstructure: "Prestige," "impact factor," "peer review standards" justify system
Contradiction: Open knowledge production captured by profit-seeking middlemen
Revolutionary potential: Open access movement, researcher-owned platforms

LIMITATIONS:
- Can be reductive: not everything reduces to economic base
- Overestimates role of class vs. other identities (race, gender)
- Predicts revolutions that don't always occur
- Weak on explaining why individuals act against class interest
- Struggles with post-industrial information economy
- Cultural factors may be more independent than base/superstructure allows
- Historical determinism too strong

ISOMORPHIC PATTERNS TO RECOGNIZE:
Look for: producers vs. owners, labor vs. capital, value extraction, ideological justification, contradictions between stated purpose and actual function, systems that benefit those who control resources while claiming to benefit all
```

**EXAMPLE - FREUDIAN PSYCHOANALYTIC_CONCEPTS.TXT:**
```
AUTHOR: Sigmund Freud
FRAMEWORK: Psychoanalytic Theory

CORE ANALYTICAL STRUCTURE:
Topographical model (early): Conscious / Preconscious / Unconscious
Structural model (later): Id / Ego / Superego
Dynamic model: Psychic energy (libido), cathexis, investment, discharge
Developmental model: Oral, Anal, Phallic, Latency, Genital stages
Interpretive model: Manifest vs. Latent content

KEY CONCEPTS:
- Unconscious: Repressed wishes, memories, drives outside awareness
- Repression: Primary defense - pushing unacceptable content out of consciousness
- Id: Primitive drives (sex, aggression), pleasure principle, primary process
- Ego: Mediator between id, superego, reality; reality principle, secondary process
- Superego: Internalized parental/social prohibitions, conscience, ego-ideal
- Libido: Sexual/life drive energy
- Death drive (Thanatos): Aggressive, destructive energy
- Oedipus complex: Childhood sexual feelings toward opposite-sex parent, rivalry with same-sex parent
- Castration anxiety: Boy's fear of punishment for Oedipal wishes
- Penis envy: Girl's perceived lack and wish for male anatomy
- Fixation: Arrested development at psychosexual stage
- Regression: Return to earlier developmental stage under stress
- Transference: Patient projects feelings onto analyst
- Countertransference: Analyst's feelings toward patient
- Resistance: Defenses against therapeutic insight
- Defense mechanisms: Strategies to manage anxiety
  - Repression: Pushing into unconscious
  - Projection: Attributing own feelings to others
  - Reaction formation: Expressing opposite of true feeling
  - Sublimation: Channeling drives into acceptable outlets
  - Displacement: Redirecting feelings to safer target
  - Rationalization: Logical excuse for emotional motive
  - Denial: Refusing to acknowledge reality
- Dream-work: Condensation, displacement, symbolization, secondary revision
- Free association: Unfiltered speech reveals unconscious
- Parapraxes: Slips of tongue, forgetting reveal unconscious
- Symptom formation: Compromise between wish and defense
- Cathexis: Investment of psychic energy in object
- Pleasure principle: Seek pleasure, avoid pain (id)
- Reality principle: Delay gratification for practical concerns (ego)
- Primary process: Immediate wish-fulfillment, ignores logic (id, dreams)
- Secondary process: Logical, reality-oriented thinking (ego)

ANALYTICAL PATTERN:
1. Identify presenting symptoms/complaints
2. Gather biographical information, especially childhood
3. Note dreams, free associations, slips, resistances
4. Identify what's being defended against (repressed wish)
5. Identify defense mechanisms
6. Trace symptom to childhood origin (usually Oedipal)
7. Interpret symbolic meaning
8. Work through transference
9. Make unconscious conscious
10. Achieve insight leading to symptom relief

APPLICABLE DOMAINS:
- Individual psychology and psychopathology
- Dreams
- Art and literature (what does it express unconsciously?)
- Religion (illusion, father-figure projection)
- Culture and civilization (repression required)
- Humor (release of repressed aggression)
- Mythology (collective fantasy)
- Biography (explain person's life through unconscious)
- Social movements (mass psychology)
- Any human behavior or production

CHARACTERISTIC QUESTIONS:
- What is being repressed here?
- What childhood trauma or conflict does this trace to?
- What wish is being defended against?
- How is this symptom a compromise formation?
- What does this symbolize?
- Why is the person resisting this interpretation?
- What Oedipal dynamics are at play?
- How does this relate to sexual or aggressive drives?
- What defense mechanisms are operating?
- What would free association reveal?

EXAMPLE APPLICATIONS:

EXAMPLE 1 - PERFECTIONISM:
Presenting issue: Person has crippling perfectionism, can't complete projects
Childhood origin: Critical, demanding father; fear of never being good enough
Oedipal dynamics: Trying to win father's approval, compete with father
Repressed wish: To be recognized as superior to father
Defense: Reaction formation - "I'm not trying to surpass father, I'm trying to please him"
Symptom: Perfectionism ensures nothing is ever finished, avoiding test of actual superiority
Symbolic meaning: Incomplete projects = staying child, not competing as adult
Transference: Treats therapist as critical judge
Interpretation: Perfectionism protects against Oedipal rivalry by ensuring failure

EXAMPLE 2 - POLITICAL FANATICISM:
Presenting issue: Extreme devotion to political ideology, intolerance of dissent
Childhood origin: Authoritarian father, strict rules, punishment for disobedience
Repressed wish: To rebel against father, express rage at being controlled
Defense: Identification with the aggressor - becoming the authority figure
Symptom: Rigid adherence to ideology, punishing "heretics"
Symbolic meaning: Ideology = father figure; dissenters = rebellious child self
Superego dynamics: Harsh superego taken from father now applied to political sphere
Interpretation: Political fanaticism is displacement of unresolved father conflict

EXAMPLE 3 - WRITER'S BLOCK:
Presenting issue: Established writer suddenly can't write
Recent event: Success of previous book
Unconscious conflict: Success triggers castration anxiety (surpassing father)
Repressed wish: To be recognized as genius (Oedipal triumph)
Defense: Inhibition of writing prevents realization of wish
Symptom: Block protects against feared punishment for success
Oedipal dynamics: Writing = phallic achievement; block = symbolic castration
Resistance: When therapist suggests this interpretation, patient insists it's just external stress
Interpretation: Block is compromise - maintains identity as writer while preventing actual success

LIMITATIONS:
- Unfalsifiable: Resistance to interpretation proves interpretation correct
- Overemphasizes sexual/Oedipal factors
- Male-centered (penis envy, etc.)
- Limited sample: Neurotic Viennese bourgeoisie
- Ignores social/cultural/economic factors
- Some concepts (death drive) speculative
- Difficult to test empirically
- Therapeutic efficacy debated
- Modern neuroscience challenges some premises

ISOMORPHIC PATTERNS TO RECOGNIZE:
Look for: Surface vs. hidden meaning, expressed vs. defended-against wishes, childhood origins of adult patterns, symbolic expressions, resistance to insight, compromise formations, energy investment/withdrawal, primary vs. secondary process thinking, pleasure vs. reality demands
```

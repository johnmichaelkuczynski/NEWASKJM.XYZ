BERTRAND RUSSELL POSITION STATEMENTS 

POSITION STATEMENTS: RUSSELL - THE ANALYSIS OF MATTER (1927)

1. Physics tells us only about the structure of the physical world, not about its intrinsic nature.

2. Our knowledge of the external world is entirely structural or relational, never qualitative.

3. Events, not substances or particles, are the fundamental constituents of the physical world.

4. Percepts are a subset of the events that constitute the brain.

5. Naive realism is false: our percepts do not directly reveal the intrinsic qualities of external objects.

6. The only things we know non-inferentially are our own percepts and their qualities.

7. Physical objects are logical constructions out of events.

8. Causation is to be understood in terms of functional relations between events, not as a metaphysical power.

9. The distinction between mind and matter is not fundamental but reflects different causal arrangements of neutral events.

10. What physics describes as matter is really groups of events connected by causal laws.

11. Space-time structure is knowable through physics, but the intrinsic character of what occupies space-time is not.

12. Inference from percepts to their external causes is justified by the principle of causal continuity.

13. The qualities we directly experience (colors, sounds, etc.) exist only in percepts, not in external objects.

14. Physical space and perceptual space are distinct; physics deals only with the former.

15. The electron and other theoretical entities are logical fictions constructed from groups of events.

16. The causal theory of perception is the correct account of how we perceive external objects.

17. All empirical knowledge of the world beyond our percepts depends on causal inference.

18. The "matter" of physics is far more abstract than common sense supposes.

19. There is no good reason to believe that physical objects have any intrinsic nature beyond their structural properties.

20. The world described by physics is not the world of everyday experience but a mathematical abstraction.

21. Neutral monism provides a more economical ontology than either materialism or idealism.

22. Mind and brain are different aspects of the same underlying neutral events.

23. The only intrinsic qualities we know are the qualities of our own mental states.

24. Modern physics (relativity and quantum mechanics) supports a structural rather than substantival view of matter.

25. Space and time are not independent realities but aspects of the relations among events.

26. Perception involves a causal chain from external object to brain event, and the percept occurs at the end of that chain.

27. We cannot know whether the intrinsic nature of external events resembles the intrinsic nature of our percepts.

28. The common-sense distinction between "real" and "apparent" properties cannot be maintained.

29. Scientific inference from percepts to physics requires assuming that similar structures have similar causes.

30. The physical world is not directly observable; all observation is of percepts.

31. Traditional materialism errs in ascribing to matter qualities that belong only to percepts.

32. Physical objects do not have color, sound, smell, or any secondary qualities in themselves.

33. The brain is part of the physical world, and mental events are identical with certain brain events.

34. Our knowledge of physics is hypothetical knowledge about structure, confirmed by its predictive success.

35. The distinction between physics and psychology is a difference in the causal laws studied, not in the entities involved.

36. All that physics can legitimately assert about matter is its mathematical-structural properties.

37. The continuity of nature suggests that the intrinsic character of events outside brains is not wholly unlike percepts.

38. There is no dualistic gap between mind and matter because both reduce to events.

39. Substance is a metaphysical illusion; what exists is patterns of events.

40. The inference from percepts to external causes is probabilistic, not demonstrative.

41. Scientific realism must be reformulated as structural realism rather than naive realism about theoretical entities.

42. Quantum mechanics reveals that the classical picture of determinate particles is false.

43. The physical world is a four-dimensional manifold of events related by causal laws.

44. Perception gives us direct knowledge only of the terminus of a causal process, not of its origin.

45. The principle of spatiotemporal continuity in causation underwrites inference from effect to cause.

46. Mental causation is a species of physical causation involving brain events.

47. Epistemological dualism (between percepts and objects) does not entail ontological dualism (between mind and matter).

POSITION STATEMENTS: RUSSELL - THE ANALYSIS OF MIND (1921)

1. Mind and matter are both logical constructions out of a neutral stuff that is neither mental nor physical in itself.

2. Consciousness is not a unique substance or entity but a particular arrangement of neutral elements.

3. Sensations are the intersection point where physics and psychology meet; they belong to both domains.

4. Introspection is not a separate faculty revealing a private mental realm but simply attention directed at certain events.

5. The subject or ego is not a substantive entity but a logical fiction constructed from experiences.

6. Belief is a quality of certain mental occurrences, not a relation between a mind and a proposition.

7. Desire is to be analyzed in terms of behavior and discomfort cycles, not as a primitive mental act.

8. Images differ from sensations in their causes and effects, not in their intrinsic nature.

9. Memory is not direct acquaintance with the past but present images accompanied by a feeling of pastness.

10. Meaning is constituted by the causal relations between words and what they signify, not by mental acts of meaning.

11. Words acquire meaning through association with objects and situations, not through private definitions.

12. Thought can occur without language, though language greatly extends its scope and precision.

13. There is no act of consciousness distinct from its objects; consciousness is adverbial, not substantival.

14. The traditional distinction between mental acts and their contents is spurious.

15. Behaviorism is partially correct: much of what we call mental can be analyzed in behavioral terms.

16. Behaviorism is partially incorrect: it cannot account for images, which are genuine mental particulars.

17. Knowledge by acquaintance is limited to present sensations and images.

18. Knowledge of the external world and other minds is entirely inferential.

19. Propositions are not entities; what exists are propositional attitudes, which are complex psychological facts.

20. Imagination consists of images that are copies of past sensations, operating under different causal laws.

21. The distinction between true and false beliefs lies in their correspondence or non-correspondence with facts.

22. Emotions are complex states involving sensations, images, and bodily feelings.

23. Will is not a special faculty but desire plus belief that action will achieve the desired end.

24. Habit, not reason, governs most human behavior.

25. Instinct is inherited habit, modified by experience but not created by it.

26. Perception is not passive reception but active construction involving inference and interpretation.

27. The "self" is a series of experiences connected by memory and causal continuity, not a persisting substance.

28. There is no pure ego or transcendental subject underlying experience.

29. Mental events are distinguished from physical events by their causal laws, not by their intrinsic nature.

30. Mnemic causation (where past events influence present behavior without intermediate links) is fundamental to psychology.

31. Association of ideas is a real psychological law but does not fully explain the structure of thought.

32. Attention is selective response, not a special mental power directed by a homunculus.

33. Pleasure and pain are qualities of sensations, not separate mental entities.

34. The "intentionality" of mental states (their aboutness) reduces to causal and associative relations.

35. Language is a social phenomenon; meaning is public, not private.

36. Understanding a word consists in having appropriate images or behavioral responses associated with it.

37. General ideas are not abstract entities but habits of response to similarities among particulars.

38. Judgment is a complex of sensations, images, and feelings, not a unique mental act relating a mind to a proposition.

39. Truth is a property of beliefs, consisting in their correspondence with facts.

40. Facts are not propositions but arrangements of particulars that make propositions true or false.

41. The unconscious exists: mental causation can occur without conscious awareness.

42. Dreams are wish-fulfillments, as Freud claimed, though his specific theories require modification.

43. The analysis of mind requires both introspective and behavioral data; neither alone suffices.

44. Physics and psychology study the same neutral events from different perspectives and with different groupings.

45. There are no purely mental substances; the mind is a construction out of events that also constitute the body.

46. Personal identity over time is a matter of degree, not an all-or-nothing affair.

47. The feeling of familiarity is a distinct mental quality that can attach to any object of experience.

48. Vagueness is essential to most thought and language; precision is the exception, not the rule.

POSITION STATEMENTS: RUSSELL - THE PRINCIPLES OF MATHEMATICS (1903)

1. Pure mathematics is the class of all propositions of the form "p implies q" where p and q contain only logical constants and variables.

2. Mathematics and logic are identical; mathematics is simply logic developed to a higher degree.

3. All mathematical concepts can be defined in terms of purely logical concepts.

4. All mathematical truths can be derived from purely logical axioms.

5. The natural numbers can be defined as classes of similar classes.

6. Zero is the class of all empty classes.

7. One is the class of all unit classes.

8. A cardinal number is the class of all classes similar (equinumerous) to a given class.

9. Similarity between classes is defined by the existence of a one-to-one correspondence.

10. The successor relation among numbers is definable in purely logical terms.

11. Mathematical induction is derivable from the definition of natural numbers, not an independent axiom.

12. Infinity is a logical possibility and presents no inherent contradiction.

13. The actual infinite exists in mathematics; potential infinity is an inadequate substitute.

14. Fractions and real numbers can be constructed from natural numbers using logical operations.

15. Real numbers are best defined as segments (Dedekind cuts) of the rational number series.

16. Continuity is a property of series, definable without appeal to intuition.

17. The calculus can be made rigorous through the arithmetization of analysis; infinitesimals are eliminable.

18. Limits involve no metaphysical notion of "approaching"; they are definable in static terms.

19. Geometry is a branch of pure mathematics when studied abstractly, apart from physical space.

20. Projective geometry is more fundamental than metric geometry.

21. Space as studied by geometry is not necessarily physical space.

22. Different geometries (Euclidean, non-Euclidean) are equally consistent; which describes physical space is empirical.

23. Points, lines, and planes are logical constructions, not intuited simples.

24. Order is a fundamental logical concept not reducible to anything simpler.

25. Asymmetrical relations are essential to mathematics and cannot be eliminated.

26. Relations are as fundamental as classes; neither can be reduced to the other.

27. A relation-in-intension is distinct from the class of ordered pairs that constitutes its extension.

28. Serial order depends on transitive, asymmetrical, and connected relations.

29. The concept of "between" is fundamental to the theory of order.

30. Dense series (between any two terms there is another) are central to analysis.

31. Compact series and continuous series must be carefully distinguished.

32. Cantor's theory of transfinite numbers is logically sound and mathematically indispensable.

33. There are different sizes of infinity; some infinite sets are larger than others.

34. The power set of any set has greater cardinality than the original set.

35. The continuum is larger than the set of natural numbers (uncountably infinite).

36. Ordinal numbers differ from cardinal numbers and are essential for well-ordered sets.

37. A well-ordered set is one in which every non-empty subset has a least element.

38. The theory of types is needed to avoid logical contradictions.

39. Classes and individuals belong to different logical types.

40. No class can be a member of itself without generating contradiction.

41. The class of all classes is an illegitimate totality.

42. Propositions about "all propositions" must be interpreted with care to avoid paradox.

43. Russell's paradox (the class of all classes not members of themselves) shows naive set theory is inconsistent.

44. Denoting concepts are essential to understanding how general propositions work.

45. "All," "some," "any," and "the" function differently and must be carefully distinguished.

46. Definite descriptions ("the so-and-so") denote even when nothing satisfies them (in a sense).

47. Propositional functions are more fundamental than propositions.

48. A propositional function becomes a proposition when a value is assigned to its variable.

49. Variables range over types; unrestricted variables lead to paradox.

50. Implication is the fundamental logical relation from which others are derivable.

51. Material implication differs from formal implication (which involves variables).

52. Logical constants include implication, negation, disjunction, and the notion of a propositional function.

53. Propositions are objective entities, not mental acts or sentences.

54. True and false are indefinable properties of propositions.

55. Facts are complexes consisting of terms united by relations.

56. Every proposition is either true or false (bivalence holds).

57. Identity is a relation, though a peculiar one that holds only between a thing and itself.

58. Classes are logical fictions; talk of classes is reducible to talk of propositional functions.

59. The null class exists and is unique.

60. The universal class for a given type includes all entities of that type.

61. Class membership and class inclusion are fundamentally different relations.

62. Arithmetic can be derived entirely from logic without appeal to intuition.

63. Peano's axioms for arithmetic can be proven from logical definitions.

64. Addition is definable as an operation on cardinal numbers without appeal to intuition.

65. Multiplication is definable logically in terms of one-many relations.

66. Exponentiation is the number of ways of correlating two classes.

67. Subtraction and division are not always possible within the natural numbers; this motivates extensions.

68. Negative numbers and rationals are constructed as ordered pairs of natural numbers.

69. Zero and one are not successive in any metaphysically deep sense; their relation is logical.

70. Mathematical existence means logical consistency; no other existence is required.

71. Existence in mathematics is not spatio-temporal existence.

72. Definitions in mathematics are always explicit; implicit definition is a confusion.

73. Axioms are not self-evident truths but primitive propositions from which others follow.

74. The choice of axioms is partly conventional but constrained by logical requirements.

75. Proof in mathematics is deduction from axioms and definitions using logical rules.

76. Symbolic logic is an essential tool for achieving rigor in mathematics.

77. Ordinary language is systematically misleading about logical form.

78. The subject-predicate form of ordinary grammar does not reveal logical structure.

79. Relational propositions cannot be reduced to subject-predicate form.

80. Plurality is a fundamental logical concept; monism about relations is false.

81. External relations are real; not all relations are reducible to properties of their terms.

82. Bradley's regress against relations fails; relations are genuine constituents of facts.

83. Wholes are not always prior to their parts; logical atomism is defensible.

84. Analysis does not falsify; complex entities are genuinely composed of simpler ones.

85. The part-whole relation differs from set membership and must not be confused with it.

86. Extensive magnitude is analyzable in terms of the part-whole relation.

87. Intensive magnitude presents philosophical difficulties not fully resolved by logic alone.

88. Motion is analyzable as a function from times to positions; Zeno's paradoxes are soluble.

89. Zeno's paradoxes rest on confusions about infinity and continuity that modern analysis resolves.

90. Time, like space, can be represented by a continuous series of instants.

91. Instants are logical constructions from events or other temporal relations.

92. Change is analyzable as different properties at different times; no contradiction arises.

93. Causation is not a logical relation; it is not part of pure mathematics.

94. Necessity in mathematics is logical necessity, not causal or metaphysical necessity.

95. Modality (possibility, necessity) in mathematics reduces to consistency and derivability.

96. Nominalism about universals is false; relations and properties are genuine entities.

97. Universals exist independently of their instances; Platonism about mathematical objects is correct.

98. Classes, numbers, and relations are abstract objects with genuine being.

99. Philosophy must use the methods of logic and analysis, not speculation or intuition.

100. The foundations of mathematics require philosophical clarification that only logic can provide.

49. The word "mental" should be defined by the causal laws governing certain events, not by reference to a special substance.

50. Cognition is not a unique relation between mind and object but a complex of causally connected events.

51. The "given" in experience is not raw sensation but already contains interpretive elements.

52. Perception involves habits built up from past experience that shape how we respond to stimuli.

53. There is no sharp boundary between perception and hallucination; both are causally explicable.

54. The external reference of sensations is not intrinsic to them but added by belief and habit.

55. Sensations do not inherently point beyond themselves to external objects; this reference is constructed.

56. Images can be mistaken for sensations, particularly in cases of vivid imagination or hallucination.

57. The privacy of mental life is exaggerated; much of what we call private is publicly inferrable.

58. Other minds are known by analogy with our own case, supported by behavioral evidence.

59. Animals possess minds in the same sense humans do; the difference is one of degree, not kind.

60. Infant minds develop through the gradual organization of initially chaotic sensations.

61. Expectation is a present mental state with future reference, analyzable without postulating future entities.

62. Surprise occurs when expectations built from habit are violated by present sensation.

63. Recognition is a present feeling, not a comparison between present perception and stored memory image.

64. The feeling of knowing differs from actual knowledge; one can feel certain yet be wrong.

65. Concepts are not abstract objects grasped by intellect but habits of using words similarly.

66. Abstract ideas are verbal habits, not mental pictures of general qualities.

67. The meaning of a word is not an image but the entire system of reactions the word produces.

68. Sentences, not words, are the primary units of meaning.

69. A word in isolation has only "dictionary meaning"; full meaning requires sentential context.

70. Logical words ("not," "or," "all") cannot be understood through images; their meaning is functional.

71. Propositional attitudes differ in their behavioral and physiological manifestations, not in relating to different abstract entities.

72. Doubt is a genuine mental state distinct from both belief and disbelief.

73. Questioning is a mental state with its own distinctive feeling-tone and behavioral expression.

74. Error arises from treating images as sensations or from inappropriate habitual inferences.

75. Negative facts do not exist; negation is a feature of propositions and beliefs, not of reality.

76. True beliefs are those whose behavioral consequences succeed; false beliefs lead to surprise and frustration.

77. Inference is primarily a process of habit, not a conscious application of logical rules.

78. Reasoning is serial association constrained by habitual patterns of connection.

79. Logic describes ideal inference; psychology describes actual inference, which often departs from the ideal.

80. Instinct is modified by experience much more readily in humans than in lower animals.

81. The greater plasticity of human instinct explains the greater role of learning in human life.

82. Imitation is a fundamental mechanism by which habits spread through a community.

83. Sympathy is the tendency to feel emotions similar to those we perceive in others.

84. Social feeling develops from primitive sympathy modified by experience and habit.

85. Language is the primary vehicle by which individual experience becomes social.

86. Thought without language is imagistic and imprecise; language makes abstract thought possible.

87. Inner speech (verbal imagery) is central to adult human cognition.

88. Children think in images before they think in words; verbal thought is a later development.

89. The meaning of a word for an individual depends on that individual's history of associations.

90. Definitions in ordinary language differ from definitions in logic; the former are always somewhat vague.

91. Vague beliefs can be true; precision is not required for truth.

92. Emotional associations attach to words and color our beliefs about what the words denote.

93. Propaganda works by exploiting the emotional associations of words rather than their cognitive content.

94. Desire is essentially connected with discomfort and the activity aimed at removing it.

95. The object of desire is often unknown to the desiring subject until satisfaction is achieved.

96. Purpose is analyzable in terms of desire and belief without postulating a separate faculty of will.

97. Pleasure accompanies the satisfaction of desire; it is not itself the object of desire.

98. Displeasure accompanies the frustration of desire and drives behavior aimed at alleviating it.

99. The James-Lange theory of emotion is substantially correct: emotions are largely constituted by bodily feelings.

100. Fear, anger, and other emotions are best understood as patterns of bodily sensation plus behavioral tendency.

101. Consciousness admits of degrees; there is no sharp line between conscious and unconscious processes.

102. The unconscious influences behavior through habits and associations of which we are unaware.

103. Forgetting is not the destruction of memory traces but the inhibition of their influence.

104. Repression is a real phenomenon; some memories are actively prevented from influencing consciousness.

105. The "censor" of psychoanalysis can be understood in terms of conflicting habit systems.

106. Dreams draw on desires that are suppressed during waking life.

107. The apparent irrationality of dreams reflects the loosening of habitual constraints on association.

108. Sleep reduces the influence of present sensation, allowing memory and imagination freer play.

109. Attention involves the selective enhancement of certain stimuli and the inhibition of others.

110. Concentration is sustained attention maintained by interest or effort.

111. Fatigue in mental work is primarily fatigue of attention, not of any cognitive faculty.

112. The stream of consciousness is not a single unified flow but a complex of interacting processes.

113. The feeling of activity is a genuine component of experience, accompanying effortful mental work.

114. Conation (striving) is a real feature of mental life, not reducible to mere sensation.

115. The self is not the cause of mental processes but their result; it is constructed, not given.

116. Self-consciousness is awareness of certain mental events, not awareness of a substantial ego.

117. The unity of mind is functional, not substantial; it consists in the integration of habits.

118. Split personality cases show that the unity of mind can break down; it is not metaphysically guaranteed.

119. Personal identity is constituted by memory continuity and bodily continuity, not by a soul.

120. Death is the termination of the causal series that constitutes a mind; no further existence is implied.

121. Sensations are caused by stimuli external to the nervous system; images are caused by processes within it.

122. The distinction between sensation and image is causal, not introspectively infallible.

123. Centrally-caused images and peripherally-caused sensations may be qualitatively indistinguishable.

124. Hallucinations are images mistaken for sensations due to their abnormal vividness or context.

125. The prototype of all images is the memory image, which copies past sensation.

126. Imagination operates by recombining elements derived from past sensations.

127. Creative imagination does not produce genuinely new elements but only novel combinations.

128. The laws of association (contiguity, similarity) govern the sequence of images in thought.

129. Association by contiguity is the fundamental law; association by similarity derives from it.

130. Similarity itself is not a simple relation but depends on partial identity of constituents.

131. Conditioned reflexes (Pavlov) illustrate the mechanism of association in its simplest form.

132. Learning is the modification of behavior through the formation of new associative connections.

133. Knowledge of universals reduces to the habit of responding similarly to similar particulars.

134. There are no abstract ideas over and above particular images used in a general way.

135. Berkeley was right that all ideas are particular; he erred in thinking this undermines general knowledge.

136. Generality resides in the use of an image, not in any intrinsic property of the image itself.

137. Words can function as substitutes for images, serving the same role in thought.

138. Much adult thought proceeds via words alone, without accompanying imagery.

139. Verbal thinking is more precise and controllable than imagistic thinking.

140. The behaviorist can explain verbal behavior but not the images that accompany it.

142. Introspective reports are behavioral data; they do not give privileged access to a private realm.

144. The existence of images is established by their causal efficacy, not by introspective certainty.

145. After-images prove that sensory experience can occur without external stimuli.

146. Eidetic imagery in some individuals shows that images can have sensory vividness.

147. The difference between imagining and perceiving lies in causal context, not phenomenal quality.

148. Beliefs are true when they are suitably related to facts that would verify them.

150. The pragmatic theory of truth captures something important but must be refined.

151. Truth is not constituted by usefulness but is reliably correlated with it.

152. False beliefs tend to produce actions that fail, which is why truth matters practically.

153. The correspondence theory of truth is correct but must be stated in terms of structural isomorphism.

154. A belief corresponds to a fact when there is a structural similarity between belief-components and fact-components.

155. Facts are not propositions but complexes of particulars in relation.

156. Negative propositions are made true by positive facts plus the non-existence of certain other facts.

157. General propositions are made true by conjunctions of particular facts.

158. Existence-propositions assert that a propositional function is sometimes true.

159. Knowledge is a species of belief but requires additional conditions beyond truth.

160. Knowledge requires appropriate causal connection between belief and fact.

161. True belief accidentally arrived at does not constitute knowledge.

162. Degrees of belief are psychologically real and influence behavior accordingly.

163. Assertion expresses a high degree of belief; questioning expresses suspended belief.

164. Memory-knowledge is as direct as any knowledge can be, though not infallible.

165. The pastness of a remembered event is not a relation we perceive but a quality we feel.

166. Memory can be mistaken about whether an event occurred, not just about its details.

167. Deja vu illustrates that the feeling of familiarity can attach to genuinely novel experiences.

168. Recognition-memory differs from recall; one can recognize without being able to recall.

169. Habit-memory (knowing how) differs from explicit memory (knowing that).

170. Skills are retained in habit-memory even when explicit memory of learning them is lost.

171. The past is not directly present to memory; what is present is a present image with past reference.

172. The reference to the past is constituted by belief, not by any intrinsic feature of the memory-image.

173. Expectation involves present imagery with future reference, structured analogously to memory.

174. The future-reference of expectation is also constituted by belief attached to present images.

175. Temporal orientation (past, present, future) is imposed on experience by belief, not directly sensed.

176. The specious present is the span of time experienced as "now," varying with attention and context.

177. Succession within the specious present is directly experienced; succession beyond it is inferred.

178. The feeling of duration is a genuine datum, though judgments of duration are often inaccurate.

179. Time perception depends on rhythm, change, and the fullness or emptiness of experienced content.

180. Boredom lengthens experienced time; interest shortens it.

181. Perception is never instantaneous; it always occupies a finite span of the specious present.

182. The construction of objective time from experienced temporal relations is a logical achievement.

183. Temporal relations in physics are inferred from, not directly given in, experience.

184. The privacy of temporal experience contrasts with the publicity of physical time.

185. Spatial perception is constructed from sensory cues interpreted through habit.

186. The correlation of visual, tactile, and kinaesthetic data creates the perception of external space.

187. Depth perception is learned, not innate; it results from experience with movement and touch.

188. The thing seen and the thing touched are identified through habitual correlation, not direct perception.

189. Perceptual constancy (size, shape, color) results from habitual interpretation, not raw sensation.

190. We perceive things, not sensations; sensations are discovered by subsequent analysis.

191. Common sense objects are logical constructions from sensory particulars.

192. The common sense world is a pragmatic construction useful for action, not a metaphysical given.

193. Science reconstructs the common sense world using inferences from sensory data.

194. Neither common sense nor science gives the ultimate reality; both are constructions.

195. Philosophy must analyze constructions to discover the elements from which they are built.

196. The method of logical analysis reveals the structure hidden beneath ordinary speech and thought.

197. Analysis does not destroy its object but clarifies its components and relations.

198. Synthesis from analyzed elements recreates the original whole with added understanding.

101. The notion of "class" is fundamental to mathematics and cannot be defined in terms of anything more primitive.

102. A class is wholly determined by its members; classes with the same members are identical.

103. The null class is not nothing; it is a genuine class that happens to have no members.

104. The class of all entities is a legitimate notion within any single type but not across types.

105. Intensional and extensional definitions of classes must be carefully distinguished.

106. Two propositional functions may be materially equivalent yet intensionally distinct.

107. The extension of a propositional function is the class of arguments satisfying it.

108. Predicates do not name classes; the relation between predicate and class is indirect.

109. Not every grammatical predicate corresponds to a genuine propositional function.

110. Self-referential propositional functions must be excluded to avoid paradox.

111. The hierarchy of types applies to propositional functions as well as to classes.

112. A propositional function cannot take itself as argument without type violation.

113. Quantification ("all," "some") ranges over a determinate type, not over everything whatsoever.

114. Unrestricted quantification leads to the paradoxes that afflict naive logic.

115. The theory of types is the only known solution to the logical paradoxes.

116. The vicious circle principle forbids defining an entity in terms of a totality containing it.

117. Impredicative definitions are those violating the vicious circle principle and must be rejected.

118. The ancestral relation (transitive closure) is definable in purely logical terms.

119. Mathematical induction is a consequence of defining natural numbers via the ancestral of succession.

120. Finite and infinite are definable without appeal to intuition or counting.

121. A class is infinite if and only if it is similar to a proper subclass of itself (Dedekind's definition).

122. Finite classes cannot be put in one-to-one correspondence with proper subclasses.

123. The axiom of infinity asserts the existence of at least one infinite class.

124. Whether the axiom of infinity is a logical truth is uncertain; it may be an empirical hypothesis.

125. The existence of infinite classes cannot be proven from pure logic alone.

126. Denumerable infinity (aleph-null) is the smallest infinite cardinal.

127. The rational numbers are denumerable; they can be counted by the natural numbers.

128. The real numbers are non-denumerable; no enumeration can exhaust them.

129. Cantor's diagonal argument proves the non-denumerability of the continuum.

130. The power of the continuum exceeds the power of the natural numbers.

131. Whether there is an intermediate infinity between aleph-null and the continuum is undecided (continuum hypothesis).

132. Ordinal arithmetic differs from cardinal arithmetic; order matters for ordinals.

133. The ordinal of the natural numbers is omega, the first transfinite ordinal.

134. Ordinals beyond omega are generated by the successor operation and limit operations.

135. Well-ordering is essential to the theory of ordinals; not all sets can be well-ordered without the axiom of choice.

136. The axiom of choice asserts that for any collection of non-empty sets, a selection function exists.

137. The axiom of choice is independent of the other axioms; its status is philosophically controversial.

138. Multiplicative axiom and axiom of choice are equivalent formulations of the same principle.

139. Many important mathematical theorems depend on the axiom of choice.

140. Zermelo's well-ordering theorem follows from the axiom of choice.

141. The theory of selections and the theory of relations are intimately connected.

142. A relation is asymmetric if it never holds both ways between distinct terms.

143. A relation is transitive if whenever it holds from a to b and b to c, it holds from a to c.

144. A relation is connected if for any two distinct terms, it holds one way or the other.

145. Serial relations are transitive, asymmetric, and connected.

146. Every series determines an order among its terms.

147. Two series are similar if there is a one-one relation preserving order between them.

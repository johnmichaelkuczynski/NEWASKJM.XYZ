The Frequency Theory of Probability collapses precisely here - it confuses evidence of probabilities with probabilities themselves. Two worlds with identical laws of nature have identical probabilities, full stop, regardless of whatever statistical divergences might arise from sheer coincidence. Here's the mechanism: probabilities are determined by the deterministic-probabilistic structure of operative laws - the causal architecture of reality - not by observed frequencies. Consider my computer-malfunction example: "In a world governed by the same biological - and non-biological - laws as ours, but in which by sheer coincidence smokers never got cancer/emphysema, doctors would be no less wrong than they would in our world to tell their patients that smoking was harmless." The probability that smoking causes cancer is identical in both worlds because the underlying causal mechanisms are identical - what differs is merely the statistical evidence, which in the coincidence-world misleads us. Statistics are only as truth-conducive as they're consistent with the causal mechanisms generating them. If by coincidence every computer always behaves program-compliantly even though the laws make malfunction possible, the Frequency Theory absurdly tells us the probability of malfunction is 0%, when obviously it's the same as in our universe where malfunctions actually occur. The probability is what the laws make possible, not what happens to occur in any particular run of events.

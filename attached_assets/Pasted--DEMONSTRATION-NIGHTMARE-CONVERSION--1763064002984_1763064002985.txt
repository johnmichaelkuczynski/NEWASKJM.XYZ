**The Department of Epistemic Humility**

Dr. Sarah Chen received the summons on a Tuesday. "Case #4719: Violation of Protocols Regarding Certainty. Report to Department of Epistemic Humility, 9 AM Thursday."

She'd known it would come to this.

The Department had been established in 2031, three years after the Collapse. During the Collapse, the government had acted—swiftly, decisively, incorrectly. Seventeen million people displaced by a policy based on economic projections that turned out to be wrong. The backlash led to the Epistemic Humility Act: no government action could proceed unless officials could demonstrate absolute certainty. Since absolute certainty was philosophically impossible, this effectively meant no action at all.

Within a year, the philosophy had spread beyond government. Corporations adopted Epistemic Humility Policies. Universities established Departments of Epistemic Humility. By 2035, it was everywhere.

Sarah entered the hearing room Thursday morning. Three officials sat behind a curved desk. The woman in the center, Director Park, opened a folder.

"Dr. Chen. Emergency room physician. On November 3rd, you administered epinephrine to a patient experiencing anaphylaxis."

"Yes."

"Did you have absolute certainty the patient was experiencing anaphylaxis?"

"The patient had hives, difficulty breathing, and had just eaten shellfish. She reported shellfish allergy. The signs were—"

"Did you have absolute certainty?" Director Park's voice was patient.

"Medicine doesn't work with absolute certainty. We work with—"

"Yes or no, Dr. Chen."

"No, but—"

"You administered a powerful drug based on incomplete knowledge." Administrator Voss, on Park's left, read from a screen. "Epinephrine can cause heart attack, stroke, or cardiac arrest. You knowingly injected a substance with potentially fatal side effects based on probabilistic reasoning."

"She was dying."

"You believed she was dying," Commissioner Hayes corrected from Park's right. "Belief is not knowledge. The patient might have been experiencing a panic attack. Anxiety can mimic anaphylaxis symptoms. You acted with certainty you did not possess."

Sarah felt her pulse accelerate. "If I hadn't acted, she would have died."

"You believe she would have died," Park said. "But you cannot know what would have occurred in a counterfactual timeline. Perhaps her symptoms would have resolved spontaneously. Perhaps a different physician would have arrived with better diagnostic equipment. You foreclosed those possibilities through premature action."

"This is insane."

"Dr. Chen, would you say you're certain we're insane?"

The room was silent.

"Because certainty is precisely what we're here to discuss," Park continued. "November 7th: you ordered a CT scan for head trauma. November 12th: you administered naloxone to an overdose patient. November 18th: you intubated a patient in respiratory distress. Forty-seven instances in six months where you acted on incomplete information."

Voss pulled up another screen. "In each case, you could not be certain. The head trauma patient might have had a brain tumor requiring different imaging. The overdose might have been insulin shock. The respiratory distress might have been psychosomatic. You imposed your judgment on situations that required epistemic humility."

"Patients die while you deliberate about certainty."

"Patients might die," Hayes said. "You don't know they would die. That's the distinction you refuse to accept."

Park closed the folder. "Dr. Chen, help us understand. Why do you believe your judgment supersedes the principle of epistemic humility?"

"Because epistemic humility is an excuse for cowardice. Because doctors who claim they can't know anything have no business practicing medicine. Because this entire system is built on using philosophical skepticism to justify doing nothing."

"So you're claiming knowledge you don't possess," Park said. "You're claiming the freedom to act despite deterministic constraints. You're rejecting the foundations of responsible governance."

"I'm claiming I can tell when someone can't breathe."

"Based on observable symptoms. But you cannot know—truly know—the underlying cause. You cannot know—truly know—that your intervention will help rather than harm. You cannot know—truly know—anything about consciousness or the external world. These are established philosophical positions, Dr. Chen. Positions that protect society from the harm caused by overconfident actors."

Sarah looked at the three faces. Calm. Certain of their uncertainty. The logic was perfect within their framework: action requires knowledge, knowledge is impossible, therefore action is forbidden.

"You have two options," Park said. "Remedial training in epistemic humility—six months learning to preface all statements with 'I believe' or 'It appears' rather than claiming knowledge. Or transfer to the Documentation Corps, where you'll record symptoms but make no diagnostic judgments."

"And if I refuse?"

"Refusal to acknowledge the limits of knowledge is classified as Dangerous Certainty. You'd be removed from all positions requiring judgment."

Sarah thought about the patient from November 3rd. The woman had walked out of the ER three hours after the epinephrine injection. She'd sent Sarah flowers.

"I'll continue treating patients," Sarah said.

"That's not one of your options."

"I know."

Park made a note in the file. "Dr. Chen demonstrates persistent certainty regarding her own knowledge. Recommend immediate removal from medical practice."

Two attendants entered. As they led her out, Hayes added: "This isn't punishment, Dr. Chen. We're protecting patients from the harm caused by physicians who think they know things."

The door closed.

---

Six months later, Sarah worked in Records. She filed symptoms. Other people—people trained in epistemic humility—looked at those symptoms and filed reports about their uncertainty regarding diagnosis. Then they filed reports documenting their uncertainty about treatment options. Then more reports about their inability to choose between options.

Patients waited.

Last week, a man came in with appendicitis. Sarah recognized it immediately—the pain migration, the rebound tenderness, the fever. She filed the symptom report. The attending physician filed a report noting he couldn't be certain it was appendicitis versus gastroenteritis versus nothing serious. He requested a committee review. The committee met three days later, concluded they lacked sufficient certainty for surgical intervention, and recommended observation.

The man's appendix ruptured that night. He survived—barely—after emergency surgery performed by a doctor who'd been reprimanded for "acting with unwarranted certainty during a medical emergency."

Sarah heard the attending physician's response: "We followed protocol. No one can fault us for epistemic humility."

The hospital had a permanent wing now for patients awaiting certainty. Some had been there for months. Some had died there, their deaths carefully documented as "occurring during appropriate deliberation period."

Nobody was to blame. How could they be? They didn't know. They couldn't know. The philosophy protected them.

Sarah looked at her computer screen. Another symptom report to file. Chest pain, shortness of breath, radiating to left arm. She typed the symptoms into the system and hit submit.

The system assigned it to the Cardiac Uncertainty Committee. They'd meet in five days to discuss whether they had sufficient grounds to believe the patient was experiencing a heart attack, or whether that belief constituted unwarranted certainty given the philosophical impossibility of knowing external reality with certainty.

She wondered if the patient would live that long.

But that was presumptuous. She couldn't know. Nobody could.

The philosophy had won.
